# Findings from the Renewable Energy, Economic Growth, and CO₂ Emissions Analysis

**List of countries in the panel**
Australia (Aus), Canada (Can), United Kingdom (UK), and United States (USA), China, India, Indonesia, Malaysia, and the United Arab Emirates (UAE).

**Groups and classification**:
   - **Developed countries**: Australia (Aus), Canada (Can), United Kingdom (UK), and United States (USA).
   - **Developing countries**: China, India, Indonesia, Malaysia and the United Arab Emirates (UAE).
   
## Key Insights

1. **Renewable Energy Consumption (REC) and CO₂ Emissions**:
   - A negative correlation exists between REC and CO₂ emissions, indicating that countries with higher renewable energy consumption tend to have lower CO₂ emissions.
   - The regression analysis shows that an increase in REC is associated with a decrease in CO₂ emissions.

2. **Economic Growth (GDP) and CO₂ Emissions**:
   - The analysis reveals a weak negative correlation between GDP growth and CO₂ emissions, suggesting that economic growth alone does not significantly impact CO₂ emissions in the studied countries.
   - The regression model indicates that higher GDP growth is associated with lower CO₂ emissions, but the effect is relatively small.

3. **Comparative Analysis**:
   - Developing countries exhibit a stronger negative relationship between REC and CO₂ emissions compared to developed countries.
   - The model explains a higher percentage of variation in CO₂ emissions for developing countries (68.1%) than for developed countries (14.3%).

4. **Model Performance**:
   - The linear regression model demonstrates moderate predictive performance, with an R² score of 28.7% for the overall dataset.
   - Residual analysis indicates that while the model captures general trends, it struggles with specific observations, suggesting the need for additional explanatory variables.

5. **Economic Theory Interpretation**:
   - The findings can be related to the Environmental Kuznets Curve (EKC), which suggests that emissions may rise during early economic development and then fall once countries reach higher income levels and adopt cleaner technologies.
   - In this study, the weak negative relationship between GDP growth and emissions suggests that the sample may already be moving toward a decoupling phase, where economic growth is not strongly linked to higher CO2 emissions.
   - The stronger role of REC supports the idea of green growth and energy transition, where cleaner energy use helps reduce emissions while supporting economic development.
   - The stronger REC–CO₂ relationship in developing countries suggests that structural change toward renewable energy may be a more powerful emissions-reduction mechanism than GDP growth alone.
6. **Policy Implications**:

   The findings suggest that governments should prioritize REC as a core strategy for reducing CO₂ emissions while supporting sustainable economic growth. In particular, developing countries should increase investment in solar, wind, and other clean energy technologies, because REC appears to have a stronger relationship with lower CO₂ emissions in these economies. Policymakers should also strengthen regulatory frameworks, provide incentives for clean energy adoption, and improve access to green finance and technology transfer. In addition, developed countries can support developing economies through international cooperation, investment, and knowledge-sharing to accelerate the global energy transition.

## Conclusion

The analysis shows that REC plays a significant role in mitigating CO₂ emissions and supporting sustainable economic growth, particularly in developing countries. The results are consistent with economic theories of green growth and energy transition, suggesting that cleaner energy adoption can support environmental sustainability. However, the moderate predictive performance of the model indicates that additional economic, environmental, and policy-related factors may also influence emissions.
